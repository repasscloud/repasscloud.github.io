Case Study 1: Microsoft 365 Identity & Access Automation for a Multi-Brand Organisation

The Problem

A large organisation operating across multiple brands needed to manage how staff accessed communications, shared mailboxes, and distribution lists — dynamically, based on which brand each employee was representing on a given day of the week.

Staff rotated between brand responsibilities on different days. On any given day, an employee representing Brand A needed to receive that brand’s communications and have access to its shared mailboxes — and not those of the other brands they were not representing that day. The reverse applied when they switched brands mid-week.

Managing this manually was untenable. The IT team was making changes by hand each week, with inevitable errors, delays, and access inconsistencies. There was no reliable way to ensure the right people had the right access on the right days without constant manual intervention.

What We Built

We designed and delivered a scheduled Microsoft 365 automation system that:

  • Evaluates each affected employee’s brand assignment schedule at the start of each day
  • Automatically adds or removes users from the relevant distribution lists based on that day’s assignment
  • Grants or revokes shared mailbox access according to the same schedule
  • Logs every change made, with timestamps and the rule that triggered it, providing a full audit trail
  • Runs without any manual input from the IT team on a day-to-day basis

The system was built to be rule-driven and configuration-managed, meaning changes to brand rosters or scheduling rules could be made without touching the underlying automation logic.

Outcome

  • Zero manual intervention required for day-to-day distribution list and mailbox access changes
  • Access is correct every morning without IT team involvement
  • Full audit history of every access change is available for review
  • The IT team reclaimed significant time previously spent on repetitive weekly access management tasks
  • Access errors and delays caused by manual handling were eliminated

Case Study 2: Finance Workflow Automation — Petty Cash Processing Across 2,000+ Locations

The Problem

A large organisation operating 200+ brands across more than 2,000 locations in Australia, New Zealand, and Singapore had a significant manual finance burden in its accounts payable operations.

Every day, petty cash transactions from across all locations needed to be tallied, categorised, and entered into the organisation’s Oracle HCM finance system. This required staff to:

  • Manually collect transaction records and receipts from locations
  • Identify and calculate GST and non-GST amounts for each transaction
  • Look up the correct budget codes for each employee submitting an expense
  • Consolidate everything into a daily report
  • Manually enter the results into Oracle HCM

This process consumed the full working capacity of 4 accounts payable team members every day. It was repetitive, error-prone, and offered no scalability as the organisation grew.

What We Built

We designed and delivered an end-to-end finance automation system consisting of:

A structured web form for capturing petty cash submissions at the point of entry, including:

  • Transaction details and amounts
  • Upload of receipt images or payment confirmation screenshots
  • Automatic GST and non-GST amount identification and calculation

An identity-driven budget code lookup that queries each submitting employee’s record in Active Directory / Entra ID to retrieve the correct organisational budget codes automatically — eliminating manual lookup and reducing miscoding errors.

An automated daily consolidation engine that:

  • Collects all submissions received throughout the day
  • Categorises and validates each transaction
  • Resolves budget codes from identity data
  • Generates a structured daily finance report at 6:00 PM each day, ready for finance team review

An Oracle HCM-ready output formatted for direct upload into the organisation’s finance system, removing the need for manual data entry.

Outcome

  • 4 full-time accounts payable roles freed from their primary daily duty, enabling redeployment to higher-value work
  • Daily finance consolidation across 200+ brands and 2,000+ locations in three countries now runs automatically
  • GST calculations are consistent and accurate across all submissions
  • Budget code assignment is identity-driven and reliable, reducing miscoding
  • Finance team receives a clean, structured daily report at 6:00 PM ready for review and upload
  • The organisation gained full scalability — adding new locations or brands does not increase the manual processing burden

Case Study 3: Multi-Cloud FinOps Tooling — Identifying $2M in Savings Across a $7M USD Annual Cloud Estate

The Problem

An organisation spending approximately $7 million USD per year across AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud had no unified view of where that money was going or whether it was being spent well.

Spend had grown organically across teams, clouds, and accounts. Resources were provisioned without consistent tagging or ownership. Backup and storage retention had never been reviewed at scale. Compute was sized for peaks that had long passed. Commitments and reservations were not aligned to actual usage patterns. Nobody had a clear picture of what was waste, what was justified, and what could be safely removed or rightsized.

The organisation needed more than a billing dashboard. They needed a system that could produce findings, assign ownership, manage the decision lifecycle, and track whether savings were actually realised — not just identified.

What We Built

We designed and delivered a multi-cloud cost analysis and governance platform that operates across four layers:

1. Ingestion and normalisation The tool ingests daily billing exports, resource inventories, performance metrics, backup and snapshot metadata, storage access data, rightsizing recommendations from native cloud advisors, and commitment coverage data from all three clouds. Everything is normalised into a common canonical schema that abstracts away provider-specific naming, making findings consistent regardless of which cloud they originate from.

2. Findings engine The platform runs a structured findings engine across the normalised data, producing categorised, evidence-backed findings including:

  • Idle and underutilised compute — assessed across CPU, memory, disk, network, and uptime patterns over 30, 60, and 90-day windows
  • Oversized virtual machines — cross-referenced against newer instance families and actual demand profiles
  • Unattached and orphaned storage — disks, volumes, and file shares no longer connected to active resources
  • Object storage tiering candidates — data not accessed in 180 or 365 days identified for lifecycle movement to cheaper storage tiers
  • Backup and snapshot waste — manual backup copies, snapshots retained beyond policy, and cross-region duplicates without policy justification
  • Non-production environments running continuously — compute left running outside business hours without scheduling
  • Commitment and reservation coverage gaps — predictable workloads still running on-demand pricing
  • Anomalies and cost spikes — sudden month-on-month increases detected automatically

Each finding includes estimated monthly and annual savings, a confidence score, the evidence window, a suggested action, and an assessed business risk level.

3. Review workflow A key design principle is that identifying waste is not enough. The platform manages the full decision lifecycle for every finding:

  • Findings are assigned to resource owners
  • Teams can accept, defer, or ignore findings — but ignores require a structured reason, an approver, and an expiry date
  • Every decision is logged with a full comment and approval history
  • Ignored spend does not disappear permanently — it resurfaces for review when the ignore expires
  • Realised savings are tracked separately from identified and approved savings, so the organisation always knows what has actually been achieved versus what is still in progress

4. Reporting and governance views The platform produces two distinct reporting layers: an operational view for engineering and platform teams showing active findings, aging items, and resource-level detail; and an executive FinOps view showing total spend trends, realised savings year-to-date, forecasted pipeline savings, commitment versus on-demand ratios, and review compliance rates by team.

Savings Framework

The platform was designed to identify savings across five categories:

  • Compute rightsizing and scheduling — targeting 10–15% of compute spend through rightsizing, non-production scheduling, instance family migration, and removal of abandoned nodes
  • Storage lifecycle and cleanup — targeting 4–8% through object storage archiving, orphaned disk removal, snapshot trimming, and premium tier rationalisation
  • Commitment and reservation optimisation — targeting 5–10% by aligning reserved capacity to stable baseline usage and leaving burst workloads on-demand
  • Backup and DR optimisation — targeting 2–5% by removing duplicate manual backups, reducing unnecessary cross-region copies, and enforcing retention policies
  • Network and tooling rationalisation — targeting 1–3% by reducing inter-region data transfer and removing overlapping platform tools

Outcome2

  • A single, unified cost view across AWS, Azure, and GCP — replacing fragmented per-cloud billing reviews
  • Structured findings engine identifying actionable savings opportunities with evidence, ownership, and confidence scoring
  • Full decision workflow with audit history, expiry-enforced ignores, and accountability at team level
  • Savings ledger distinguishing identified, approved, and realised savings — preventing inflated claims
  • Monthly governance cadence embedded into the organisation’s operational rhythm
  • Target savings of approximately $2 million USD per year identified across compute, storage, backups, commitments, and waste — representing approximately 28% of total annual cloud spend
